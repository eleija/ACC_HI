Imaginarium

The Imaginarium is the primary experimental research space currently under development at the Center. It is a venue where researchers at UCSD and other institutions can propose studies and experiments that make use of our special capture and observation environment for exploring ecological natural activity in a semi-controlled space. Currently we envisage some of these studies to include: interactive problem-solving; task and attention by autistic spectrum children during assembly tasks; ideation and brainstorming on design tasks in physical space and inside virtual worlds, such as VirBELA (an immersive virtual world for global business leadership development, collaboration, networking, competition, and learning); differences in emotion and feeling between human-robot and exclusively human teams.

The activities studied focus on imagination-expanding methods that work at different time scales: problem solving in the short term; ideation and brainstorming in the medium term; coordinated teamwork on design tasks over a much longer time.

What is special:In typical psychological experiments the environment and the conditions of task performance are highly structured. Participants are thought of as subjects and their range of choice is well defined and usually constrained. A task is said to be ecologically natural if it may arise in naturally occurring contexts of activity: actual classrooms, playgrounds, in household or restaurant kitchens, in music conservatoires, dance studios, chemistry laboratories and so on. We are setting the Imaginarium up to allow people to work together naturally. These activities may span different time scales. For example, some of the imagination dependent activities we will study will operate in the short term, such as problem-solving on insight or creative tasks. Others will extend over the medium term, requiring teams to work together for several hours and filling the space with papers, annotations, pictures, whiteboard notes …. Still others may take place over a much longer time and require teams to return week after week.

The capture equipment in the space ranges from motion tracking system and laser scanners to high quality Lady Bug five-camera systems, to unobtrusive eye and head tracking, and wireless EEG headgear. These capture methods create a huge data set that requires innovative data analytics. How to cope with this vast data stream is itself a major project for us and we have been working on crowd sourcing video coupled with machine learning using SDSC’s supercomputer resources for high dimensional statistical data-mining and AI-based computation. It is our hope that by feeding analytic results back to live participants we can create a positive feedback effect that will improve individual and group imagination. For instance, we expect there are cues that can be added to the thinking context of participants that will deepen reflection and inspire novel ideas.

Based on the insights we acquire we expect to deepen our understanding of imagination and creativity to the point where we can design more classical experiments with the type of narrower, more precise hypotheses needed to test ideas at the neuropsychological level. A long-range practical goal of this research program is to develop methods for dynamically manipulating meeting (and other) environments to stimulate imagination.
